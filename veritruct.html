Quality-Gated Synthetic Data
for Regulated Industries

One filtering, masking, and evaluation methodology, deployed across two generation substrates and producing two record types — instruction fine-tuning and construction-true de-identification. Span labels correct by construction, a hybrid detector at micro-F1 0.964, and measured economics of roughly $0.58 to $3.60 per ten thousand attested records. This is the engineering blog; the complete technical whitepaper is available as a downloadable PDF.

Patrick Hill, M.S. — Founder & Principal AI/ML Systems Architect PBH Applied Systems, LLC Oklahoma City  ·  July 2026
Download the Veritruct Whitepaper (PDF) → Overview & Headline Results

A buyer evaluating a synthetic dataset for a regulated setting — clinical NLP, legal-tech, financial services — usually finds a dataset card with a row count, a license, and almost nothing else. The generation pipeline is opaque. The rejection criteria are unstated. The compliance coverage is unaudited. And for a de-identification corpus — the labeled text used to train the models that redact clinical and legal records — the labels themselves are frequently unverifiable, because a dataset labeled by the same class of tool it is meant to evaluate cannot establish ground truth. That opacity was tolerable when synthetic data was a research curiosity. It is no longer, because three forces have converged to make the provenance of training data a liability rather than a settled question.

The first is copyright. The Bartz v. Anthropic litigation produced a $1.5 billion settlement — the largest copyright settlement in U.S. history — and established the operative precedent that AI developers can be held liable for where their training data came from, not merely for whether the training itself was transformative. The second is privacy. Once scraped personal data is incorporated into model weights it cannot be selectively removed; a neural network is not a structured store you can issue a deletion against, which puts web-scraped corpora in structural tension with GDPR's right to erasure, HIPAA, and CCPA all at once. The third is economics: at per-token frontier-API rates, the iterative work of developing a quality-attested dataset becomes expensive enough to discourage doing it properly at all.

Together these define a clear need — synthetic data produced under a controlled, documented, compliance-audited methodology, with quality and provenance that survive an audit. The category itself is validated; NVIDIA disclosed that more than 98% of the data in its own alignment process is synthetic. What the open ecosystem has not built is the production layer that sits between raw generation and a deliverable a regulated buyer can actually defend. Veritruct is that layer.

01 — Architecture

One methodology, two substrates, two record types

Veritruct is a single quality-gated methodology — semantic hallucination filtering, regulation-specific compliance masking, construction-true de-identification labeling, automated evaluation, and provenance-stamped delivery — wrapped around the generation step rather than baked into the model. It is deployed along two orthogonal dimensions, and the central architectural claim is that the methodology is portable across both.

The first dimension is the substrate. Veritruct Studio runs generation locally on a single consumer GPU — an RTX 4090 — where the marginal cost of a run is electricity and iteration is effectively free, making it the substrate for development and for low-volume or flexible-timeline production. Veritruct Cloud runs the identical methodology with generation offloaded to a Modal A10G served by vLLM, where throughput is high enough to meet delivery deadlines local hardware cannot. Both import an identical shared core (veritruct_core) and differ only at the single stage where generation tokens are produced. Critically, the embedding model that drives quality scoring runs locally in both deployments, even when token generation is remote — the quality signal stays on hardware the operator controls.

The second dimension is the record type. The same methodology emits an instruction fine-tuning record — an instruction paired with a quality-gated, drift-filtered, PII-masked response — and a construction-true de-identification record — a block of text paired with span-level labels marking the exact character offset and type of every personal-data element it contains. The two record types share the generation, quality, masking, and evaluation infrastructure and diverge only in what they emit and how they are validated. Frontier generation APIs are treated throughout as a neutral cost-and-capability baseline — a legitimate way to generate raw tokens, but raw tokens are exactly what still need the entire filtering, masking, evaluation, and audit layer before they are commercially deliverable.

02 — Quality Gate

Catching drift with two signals, not one

The first gate every generated record crosses is the hallucination filter. In a synthetic corpus, a hallucination is not a factual error in the abstract — it is semantic drift between the instruction and the output. Ask for a clinical progress note, get a generic disclaimer that never addresses the patient encounter, and you have a record that teaches a model to answer the wrong question. Those records are worse than useless.

The gate measures this with embedding cosine similarity between instruction and output, using Snowflake Arctic Embed L v2.0, computed on the raw output before any masking. But it is deliberately not a simple cosine floor. A record is rejected as hallucinated only when an embedding-similarity check and a lightweight keyword-overlap check both fail. The keyword net is the safety valve: a terse, vocabulary-dense clinical or legal output that the general-purpose embedder under-scores is still kept if it shares the instruction's substantive vocabulary. Every borderline case within ±0.05 of the active threshold is logged to a per-run borderline_cases.jsonl for calibration review — the AND-rule and that logging window have been constant throughout the project. What changed is the floor the first signal is measured against.

A low cosine score alone does not reject a record. The cosine floor is a necessary but not sufficient condition for rejection — which is exactly what lets the gate recover the records a floor alone would wrongly discard.

The current committed floor is 0.45, applied broadly, with a single documented exception — legal on Cloud remains at 0.58, because legal outputs draw on more domain-specific vocabulary and warrant a stricter floor. That 0.45 landing point is itself an empirical narrative. The earlier regime used 0.55 as the default and 0.58 for medical and legal — the correct floor for the output profile at the time. Once the de-identification fixes described below began producing fully completed records rather than sparse templates, those richer outputs scored measurably lower cosine-to-instruction: a long, detailed record diverges more from its short generating instruction than a skeleton does, so the very act of fixing generation quality depressed the similarity of the good records. Under the old floor, the great majority of hallucination-flagged de-identification records sat in the 0.45–0.58 band and were, on inspection, genuinely good. Lowering the floor to 0.45 recovered them while still rejecting the genuinely off-topic outputs, which score below 0.45 and fail both signals. Because that reflects a property of completed-record output in general, 0.45 was adopted as the unified operative threshold rather than a de-identification-only override.

03 — Compliance & Labels

Masking as a gate; labels from the injection log

The second layer asks a different question: is this output safe to deliver? Compliance is enforced at generation time against named regulatory frameworks and verified on the delivered output — it is not a license note appended to a dataset card. Detected personal data is replaced with typed placeholders (<EMAIL>, <IP_ADDRESS>, <CPT_CODE>) so realism is preserved while the sensitive value is removed, and an independent, deliberately broader scanner audits the placeholder-stripped remainder of every record, biased to over-report — because a privacy metric that reuses the masker's blind spots can report "clean" while a raw value sits in plain sight.

For the de-identification record type, the labels are the product, and their provenance is the defensible core. Construction-true labeling inverts the direction of the ground truth: rather than generating text and then detecting entities in it, an injector takes gated, placeholder-bearing output and substitutes format-shaped synthetic values into the placeholder tokens. Because the injector places each value, it knows the exact range, type, and value of every entity it wrote — and it logs those as the spans. The labels are correct by construction; they come from the injection log, not from a detector, so there is no recall ceiling to inherit because nothing is being detected. A second benefit matters in regulated settings: the values are generated, never collected, so no real personal data enters the pipeline, and the construction-true span log is at once the training label and the privacy attestation.

04 — The Central Result

The template-leak gate and the yield recovery

The single most consequential engineering episode in Veritruct's development is the clearest demonstration of why the measurement infrastructure is itself the product. A general-purpose model, asked to produce a completed record, frequently produces a skeleton instead — a template studded with fill-in-the-blank stubs like [Your Name], [Admission Date], or <INSERT_PATIENT_IDS_HERE> — because a blank form is a statistically reasonable rendering of many document types. For an instruction record this is merely low quality; for a construction-true de-identification record it is fatal, because a skeleton has no narrative to learn from and its bracketed stubs are the absence of content, not labeled spans.

The defect was hidden, and the reason is the lesson. The early template-leak gate recognized only one skeleton style — angle-bracket stubs of the form <INSERT_X>. Square-bracket skeletons slipped straight through and were counted as accepted, so the run reported an apparent yield of roughly 95%. That number was false: it measured the fraction of records that lacked the one skeleton style the gate happened to check for, not the fraction that were genuinely complete. Widening the gate to recognize every skeleton style the model actually produces — while sparing legitimate typed placeholders and bracketed citations via a citation guard — collapsed the apparent yield to a true 35%. Nothing about the output had changed; the gate had simply stopped counting skeletons as complete records.

A gate that reports a false 95% gives an engineer no signal to act on; a gate that reports a true 35% defines the problem precisely. Four successive fixes then rebuilt the yield, each adopted because a number moved in response to it: a document-type change away from letters and consent forms that invite skeletonization; the 0.45 threshold recalibration above; value-packet-first generation, the root-cause fix, in which the pipeline pre-generates concrete non-PII values in code and hands them to the model to use verbatim while personal data continues to flow through span-preserving token injection; and a batched "Phase 2.5" repair pass that rewrites residual stubs and re-gates the repaired record through the identical acceptance checks. A pipeline blind to the defect would have shipped a dataset in which two-thirds of records were blank forms and reported it as 95% clean. What prevented that was not a better generation model but a more honest gate.

05 — Detector Evaluation

Grading the detector against known-correct labels

Because the de-identification labels are correct by construction, a detector scored against them grades the detector, not the labels — a low recall on a type means the detector missed spans known to be present. A hybrid regex-plus-GLiNER2 detector pass over the delivered output of the ten-thousand-record medical run, scored against 49,344 construction-true ground-truth spans, achieved a micro-averaged F1 of 0.964 (precision 0.986, recall 0.944), clearing F1 0.95 on every identifier type that carries meaningful frequency in a medical corpus: email at 1.000, date at 0.990, ICD-10 at 0.972, national identifier at 0.963, CPT code at 0.957.

The honesty of the report is in what sits beside that headline. The macro-averaged F1 on the medical run is only 0.588 — an artifact of low-support types with little or no medical-corpus frequency, not a measure of detector quality on the data that exists. A second thousand-record run spanning all seven domains populates those types and lifts the macro-average to 0.811, with fifteen of seventeen supported types clearing F1 0.96. The two that score low are the free-text entities the learned tier is uniquely responsible for — personal names and institution identifiers — where recall is high (0.957 on names) but precision is low: the detector finds the entities but over-predicts their boundaries. That is a genuine, honestly-reported weakness in the free-text tier, and because it is now measured across a populated type it is actionable in a way the medical run's empty types were not.

06 — Results

Two substrates, two record types, one quality bar

The methodology was validated on two log- and manifest-verified ten-thousand-record medical de-identification runs, one per substrate, plus separate production runs for the instruction record type. The two de-identification runs behave consistently — yield within one and a half percentage points, identical rejection ordering (hallucination dominant, then template-leak, then bias, then fidelity) — which is exactly the result the two-substrate thesis requires: the substrate is an operational choice, not a quality determinant.

07 — Economics

A time crossover, not a cost one

Because both production runs delivered the same ten-thousand-record product, their throughput and cost are a direct, measured comparison rather than a projection. The Studio run completed 10,016 records in roughly 17.84 hours at about 561 records per hour, at the measured 242-watt system draw and a blended $0.134/kWh Oklahoma City residential rate — approximately $0.58, about six cents per thousand records. The Cloud run completed 10,064 records in roughly 3.38 hours at about 2,978 records per hour, for $3.60 in metered Modal credits — about 36 cents per thousand. The Cloud substrate delivered the same dataset roughly 5.3 times faster at roughly 6.2 times the cost, and both figures are small in absolute terms.

The deeper point is a difference in cost structure, which is why both substrates are owned rather than rented from a per-token API. Owned generation imposes a fixed cost that amortizes across unbounded runs; per-token API rental imposes a linear charge that recurs on every iteration. The yield-recovery arc above consumed many full regeneration cycles across its four fixes — under owned infrastructure the marginal cost of each was cents to a few dollars, whereas under per-token pricing each would have been a fresh billable event, and the cumulative meter would have created pressure to ship the false 95% and stop. The crossover between substrates is therefore a time crossover, not a cost one: Studio is cheaper per record at every volume, so the only question per engagement is whether its throughput meets the deadline — and if not, Cloud meets it at a cost that stays a negligible fraction of the engagement's value.

08 — Limitations

Stated as plainly as the results

A methodology that asks regulated buyers to trust its output earns that trust partly by naming what it cannot guarantee. The clearest and most actionable limitation is injected value fidelity: on the medical run, 7,289 of 49,344 injected span values — about 14.8% — failed format validation for their declared type. Because the labels are correct by construction this is not a labeling error; the spans are located and typed correctly, but a minority of the injected values are not well-formed exemplars of their type. Since the values are generated in code at known positions, tightening the generators is a bounded engineering task with a metric already in the report to optimize against, and the all-domain run's 8.4% invalid rate shows the fidelity improves outside the code-heavy medical domain.

The remaining bounds are stated with the same directness. Pattern-based detection and masking cannot be proven complete — the residual leak rate is measured and independently corroborated, but a novel output shape could surface a personal-data pattern no existing rule anticipates. The thresholds are calibrated, not self-adjusting, so a new domain or model swap would require a cheap, configuration-driven recalibration. The macro-averaged detector score reflects untested low-support types rather than a quality verdict on the data that exists. And the quantitative results characterize one domain and one record type at the ten-thousand-record scale; behavior at larger scale is bounded by the per-record cost structure rather than demonstrated. None of this undercuts the core claim — one quality-gated methodology, deployed across two complementary substrates and producing two record types, yields attested, compliant, construction-true datasets at a cost and throughput that make defensible synthetic data practical for regulated industries — but it delimits it, and never conflates what is measured with what is projected.

That, finally, is the shape of the thing. The token savings are real. But the value is the cost and risk the methodology removes downstream, where a regulated buyer would otherwise have to do the verification work itself — trustworthy labels, a clean compliance posture, an auditable provenance trail — or carry it as exposure.
