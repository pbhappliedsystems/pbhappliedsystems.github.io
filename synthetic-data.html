Engineering Note  ·  Synthetic Data

One Methodology,
Two Substrates,
One Defensible Dataset

Synthetic training data is now the operating standard at the frontier. The public ecosystem that supplies it is not. Here is how we built a quality-gated pipeline that produces attested, compliance-audited datasets — and proved it runs at the same quality whether it runs on a workstation or in the cloud.

Patrick Hill, M.S. — Founder & Principal Engineer PBH Applied Systems, LLC Oklahoma City  ·  June 2026

A buyer evaluating a synthetic dataset for a regulated setting — clinical NLP, legal-tech, financial services — usually finds a model card with a row count, a license, and almost nothing else. The generation pipeline is opaque. The rejection criteria are unstated. The compliance coverage is unaudited. That opacity was tolerable when synthetic data was a research curiosity. It is no longer, because three forces have converged to make the provenance of training data a liability rather than a settled question.

The first is copyright. The Bartz v. Anthropic litigation produced a $1.5 billion settlement — the largest copyright settlement in U.S. history — and established the operative precedent that AI developers can be held liable for where their training data came from, not merely for whether training was transformative. The second is privacy. Once scraped personal data is incorporated into model weights, it cannot be selectively removed; a neural network is not a structured store you can issue a deletion against, which puts web-scraped corpora in structural tension with GDPR's right to erasure, HIPAA, and CCPA all at once. The third is economics: at per-token frontier-API rates, the iterative work of developing a quality-attested dataset becomes expensive enough to discourage doing it properly at all.

Together these define a clear need — synthetic data produced under a controlled, documented, compliance-audited methodology, with quality and provenance that survive an audit. The category itself is validated; NVIDIA disclosed that more than 98% of the data in its own alignment process is synthetic. What the open ecosystem has not built is the production layer that sits between raw generation and a deliverable a regulated buyer can actually defend.

01 — Architecture

The layer that is missing

SynthEval is that layer: a single quality-gated methodology — semantic hallucination filtering, regulation-specific compliance masking, automated evaluation, and an independent audit — wrapped around the generation step rather than baked into the model. The central architectural claim is that this methodology is portable across the thing that generates tokens. The four quality-and-compliance components run identically; only the generation substrate beneath them changes.

That portability is what makes two products out of one pipeline. SynthEval Studio runs the methodology locally on a single consumer GPU — an RTX 4090 — where the marginal cost of a run is electricity and iteration is effectively free. SynthEval Cloud runs the identical methodology with generation offloaded to a Modal A10G GPU served by vLLM, where throughput is high enough to meet delivery deadlines local hardware cannot. Critically, the embedding model that drives quality scoring runs locally in both deployments, even when token generation is remote — the quality signal stays on hardware the operator controls.

The two are not competing alternatives. They are complementary deployments of one methodology, and the choice between them is an operational decision driven by volume and timeline, not by any difference in the data they produce. Frontier generation APIs are treated throughout as a neutral cost-and-capability baseline — a legitimate way to generate raw tokens, but raw tokens are exactly what still need the entire filtering, masking, evaluation, and audit layer before they are commercially deliverable.

02 — Quality Gate

Catching drift with two signals, not one

The first gate every generated record crosses is the hallucination filter. In a synthetic instruction-tuning corpus, a hallucination is not a factual error in the abstract — it is semantic drift between the instruction and the output. Ask for a HIPAA breach-notification memo, get a generic privacy disclaimer that never mentions breach notification, and you have a record that teaches a model to answer the wrong question. Those records are worse than useless.

The gate measures this with embedding cosine similarity between instruction and output, using Snowflake Arctic Embed L v2.0. But it is deliberately not a simple cosine floor. A record is rejected as hallucinated only when an embedding-similarity check and a lightweight keyword-overlap check both fail. The keyword net is the safety valve: a terse, vocabulary-dense legal or medical output that the general-purpose embedder under-scores is still kept if it shares the instruction's substantive vocabulary. The thresholds — 0.55 for general domains, 0.58 for legal and medical — were not inherited from a paper or a prototype. They were calibrated against the embedding model's behavior on this pipeline's own instruction distribution, after an early permissive value of 0.18 proved to do no discriminating work at all.

The records the keyword net rescues are precisely the ones a cosine floor alone would wrongly discard — and the gate recovers them without weakening its rejection of genuine drift.
03 — Compliance

A gate, not a disclaimer

The second layer asks a different question: is this output safe to deliver? Compliance here is enforced at generation time against named regulatory frameworks and verified on the delivered output — it is not a license note appended to a dataset card. Detected personal data is replaced with typed placeholders (<EMAIL>, <IP_ADDRESS>, <CPT_CODE>) so realism is preserved while the sensitive value is removed. A non-negotiable always-mask floor covers the highest-sensitivity identifiers in every domain regardless of configuration, and span protection handles the messy reality of mixed records — a masked email beside a raw IP address in the same table cell, or an identifier truncated at a token boundary.

The most important property is that the leak metric does not trust its own masker. A separate, deliberately broader set of patterns scans the placeholder-stripped remainder of every delivered record, biased to over-report rather than under-report — because a privacy metric that reuses the masker's blind spots can report "clean" while a raw value sits in plain sight, and a metric that can under-report is worse than the leak it conceals. Under that independent scanner, the residual PII leak rate fell across iterations from 3.8% on the pre-hardening local run, to 0.72%, to a measured zero on the final cloud run. We state that zero precisely: measured and independently audited, never guaranteed — pattern matching cannot be proven complete.

04 — Throughput

Choosing a quantization format on evidence

Moving to the cloud only matters if it beats local on speed without losing quality. The local pipeline runs a 4-bit model at roughly 251 records per hour — fine for development, but a 20,000-record deliverable takes about five days, which fails a time-bound client SLA outright. The question was which serving format could break that wall while holding the ~76% MATTR diversity bar local development had established. 05 — Results

The finding the whole thing turns on

Across three production runs — one local, two cloud — the methodology produced equivalent datasets regardless of substrate. Lexical diversity (MATTR) held within half a percentage point. Mean instruction-output cosine similarity held within two thousandths. Near-duplicate rate was zero in all three. This parity is not luck; it is the designed consequence of holding the quality layer constant and varying only the generation step beneath it.

The one axis that is deliberately not at parity is compliance, and the difference there is an improvement rather than a substrate effect: the local run predates the masking hardening and is presented honestly as a pre-hardening baseline, while the two cloud runs were generated after span-protection and truncated-identifier detection went in. Every delivered dataset ships with an attested, machine-readable report — rejection reasons by category, scale-invariant diversity, placeholder coverage, the independently audited leak rate, and a reconciliation of the run's own counts against its manifest. That report is the verification work a buyer would otherwise have to perform or absorb as risk. It is also a working instrument, not a decorative one: during scale testing the evaluation pipeline's clustering module caught a real defect — a corpus quietly collapsing into a single medical-billing domain while every individual record still passed the gates — and drove two concrete methodology changes.

06 — Economics

A time crossover, not a cost one

Once quality is held constant, the substrate decision reduces to a single operational question per engagement: can the deliverable's deadline be met at local throughput? If it can, local wins on cost — its marginal cost is electricity. If it cannot, cloud wins on time at an absolute cost that stays a small fraction of the engagement's value. The crossover is therefore not a cost crossover — local is cheaper per record at every volume — but a time crossover. And the cloud substrate's speed is itself adjustable: it scales vertically to larger GPU tiers and horizontally across concurrent containers, with per-record cost staying roughly flat because throughput and burn rise together. (Those larger-GPU and multi-container figures are conservative projections from the measured A10G behavior, and we label them as projections, not measurements.)

The deeper economic point is that owned generation infrastructure — local or cloud — imposes a fixed cost that amortizes across unbounded runs, where per-token API rental imposes a linear charge that recurs on every iteration. Under metered pricing, every calibration pass, every hardening cycle, every regeneration carries a visible price tag that creates pressure to ship the first adequate dataset and stop. On amortized hardware you iterate until the metrics — not the budget — declare the dataset finished. The two cost structures produce different products: one sized to the cost of generating it, the other sized to the standard the publisher chose to enforce.

07 — Limitations

Stated as plainly as the results

A methodology that asks regulated buyers to trust its output earns that trust partly by naming what it cannot guarantee. Pattern-based compliance cannot be proven complete — the zero leak rate is rigorously measured and independently corroborated, but a novel output format could surface a personal-data shape no existing pattern anticipates. The hallucination thresholds are calibrated, not self-adjusting; a new domain or a model swap would require a cheap, configuration-driven recalibration. The bias-flag metric over-reports in exactly the regulated domains the methodology targets, because naming protected categories is the correct, instructed content of a faithful GDPR or legal record. And the production runs are demonstrated at the thousand-record scale; behavior at the ten-thousand-to-million-record scale is projected from that base and the per-record cost structure, with the local evaluation pipeline's super-linear memory profile a known pressure point not yet exercised in production. None of this undercuts the core claims — quality parity, the dual-signal gate's discrimination, the audited zero leak, the cost-and-throughput economics are all on measured data — but it delimits them, and never conflates what is measured with what is projected.

That, finally, is the shape of the thing. One quality-gated methodology, deployed across two complementary substrates, produces attested, compliant, equivalent datasets — cheap to develop and produce because the substrate is owned, and valuable to receive because the output is attested. The token savings are real. But the value is the cost and risk the methodology removes downstream, where a regulated buyer would otherwise have to do the verification work itself or carry it as exposure.
