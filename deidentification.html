SynthEval  ·  Engineering Blog  ·  Part II — Addendum

Labels Correct by Construction

An addendum to the SynthEval methodology. A de-identification dataset whose PII span labels are ground truth by construction rather than guessed by a detector; a gap-only learned-NER tier that recovers free-text-entity recall from zero at an F1 of 0.96; a gate that removes an emergent generation defect at the source; and the operational hardening that restored cloud cost to a measured $0.47 per thousand records. This is the engineering blog; the full technical addendum is available as a downloadable PDF.

Patrick Hill, M.S. — Founder & Principal AI/ML Systems Architect PBH Applied Systems, LLC Oklahoma City  ·  June 2026
Download Addendum II — De-identification (PDF) → ← Read Part I — Synthetic Data Blog

A buyer evaluating a de-identification dataset faces a quiet contradiction that most published corpora never resolve: the labels marking where the personal data lives were usually produced by the same kind of detector the buyer is trying to train or test. When the ground truth and the detector share a blind spot, the dataset cannot measure the one capability that matters most — whether a model finds the entities the labeler missed. This addendum to the SynthEval methodology documents the work that removes that contradiction, alongside three other developments made since the original paper. Each is reported in the same register as the first: results as measured, bounds stated plainly, and the design decisions told as the empirical narratives they were — including the one configuration that failed instructively before the right one was found.

The original SynthEval paper described a dual-substrate, quality-gated pipeline for synthetic instruction data, and nothing here revises it. The measured zero residual PII leak, the cross-substrate quality parity, and the gate behavior reported there all stand unchanged. This is an addendum by deliberate choice rather than a rewrite, for the same reason the methodology argues for everywhere else: provenance is preserved by versioned, append-only documentation, not by silently editing a released artifact that other datasets already link to. A reader meeting SynthEval for the first time should start with Part I; this is what was added afterward.

Four things changed, and they form a coherent line. A de-identification product whose labels are correct by construction. A learned-NER premium evaluation tier that closes the regex detector's documented recall gap on free-text entities. A rejection gate that removes an emergent generation defect at the only point where prevention actually works. And an operational-hardening effort that diagnosed a non-deterministic hardware fault, contained it in software, and resolved it permanently — restoring not just reliability but the measured cost structure underneath it.

01 — The Product

Labels from the injection log, not a detector

The de-identification product is a different primitive from the instruction data of the original paper. Its unit is the span-labeled pair (text, spans), where each span records the character range, type, and value of a personal-data entity in the text. That is exactly what a buyer needs to train or benchmark a de-identification model: text with known, labeled PII locations. What distinguishes the product is not the primitive but the provenance of the labels, and SynthEval supports two ways of obtaining them.

Regex-derived labeling runs the production masker's detector over generated text and records what it finds. This is honest but bounded — the labels inherit the detector's recall ceiling. Any entity the regex cannot match, such as a free-text personal name or an institution identifier with no fixed shape, is absent from both the masking and the labels. A model trained on regex-derived labels learns to find what regex already finds, and no more. The set is most useful as a faithful, free sample of format and structured-entity coverage.

Construction-true labeling inverts the direction of the ground truth. Rather than generating text and then detecting entities in it, an injector takes gated, placeholder-bearing output and substitutes format-shaped synthetic values into the placeholder tokens. Because the injector places each value, it knows the exact range, type, and value of every entity it wrote — and it logs those as the spans. The labels are correct by construction: they come from the injection log, not from a detector. There is no recall ceiling to inherit, because nothing is being detected.

A regex-labeled dataset can only ever be as complete as the regex that labeled it. Construction-true labeling breaks the coupling between the ground truth and the detector — which is the precise capability a buyer building a de-identification model most needs to evaluate.

That break is the product's defensible core, and it carries a second benefit that matters in regulated settings. The values are generated, never collected, so no real personal data enters the pipeline at any point. The construction-true span log documents exactly what synthetic PII was placed and where, which is at once the training label and the privacy attestation — and it removes the re-identification analysis a real-data de-identification corpus would otherwise require before sale. Both labeling modes emit the same record shape, with a source field on every span (injected, regex, or gliner) that makes the downstream scoring auditable span by span.

02 — The Premium Tier

Finding the right scope by failing twice first

The regex detector has a known, documented shape: near-perfect precision on structured PII — emails, IP addresses, dates, account and order identifiers, medical codes — and a recall gap on the free-text entities it cannot pattern-match, principally personal names and institution identifiers. The original paper treats that gap as a deliberate, disclosed artifact a buyer's own learned model is meant to close. The premium evaluation tier is the instrument that measures how well a learned model closes it, and it is itself built from a learned model: GLiNER2, a DeBERTaV3 encoder, loaded lazily so the base product never imports anything heavy.

Detector selection is a single configuration key, resolved through one factory so that auto-evaluation, the CLI path, and the delivery packager all score de-identification uniformly. Three modes are available:

The most consequential decision in this tier was how much of the labeling task to give the learned model — and the answer was found by measurement, through two narrowings that each removed scope. The first design let the learned model predict every entity type and merged its spans with the regex detector's. On a 107-record run this collapsed micro-precision from the regex base of roughly 0.99 to 0.70. The cause was specific: the learned model re-predicted the structured types regex already handled perfectly — at slightly different offsets, or on values regex had correctly declined — and those redundant predictions landed as false positives. Of 142 false positives in that run, 127 were on structured types the regex already detected at perfect precision. The model was not finding new errors; it was manufacturing them on solved problems.

The correction was to restrict the learned model to the types regex cannot serve and let regex own everything else. With the model requesting only gap labels, it can no longer produce a structured-type false positive, because it no longer predicts structured types at all. Precision returned to the regex base while the free-text recall gap closed. A second narrowing followed: the initial gap set still included two numeric types — account numbers and SWIFT/BIC codes — and a thousand-record run showed the learned model was simply noisier there, scoring 0.30 recall against the regex base's 0.47 on account numbers while tagging unrelated tokens as SWIFT/BIC at 0.20 precision. Those were returned to regex, where they belong. The final gap set is the two types the learned model genuinely recovers and regex genuinely cannot: personal names and institution identifiers.

A learned model earns its place in the ensemble only on the types where it strictly outperforms the deterministic detector. On this product, more coverage was worse twice before it was right.

With the gap-only hybrid, the tier was validated on full thousand-record runs across both substrates. The results are a strict improvement on F1, not a precision-for-recall trade — and they hold across substrate, which is exactly the cross-substrate parity the original paper established for generation, now shown for learned-NER evaluation too:

03 — The Template-Leak Gate

Rejecting a defect prompts cannot prevent

The gating pipeline already rejects records on hallucination, privacy, bias, GDPR-surface, URL, and fidelity grounds. This work adds one more reason, and why it was needed illustrates a general property of generation defects. Occasionally the model emits a fill-in-the-blank stub in place of content — a token like <INSERT_PATIENT_IDS_HERE> or <INSERT_URL> standing where a real value should be. These are not placeholders any template contains; the instruction never mentions them. They are emergent: asked to produce a document, the model sometimes produces a template skeleton of one instead. The defect is rare — about two records in a thousand — and that rarity is precisely what makes it hard to fix by the obvious means.

Instructing the model not to emit such tokens does not work, for three reasons worth stating because they generalize. The tokens are not in any template to remove — they originate in generation, so there is no source-side text to correct. Negative instructions of the form "never emit a token like X" are unreliable against rare emergent behavior and can prime the very pattern they forbid. And the space of possible stub forms cannot be enumerated in advance, so a prompt listing forbidden shapes is always incomplete. Prevention by prompt is the wrong instrument for a rare, emergent, open-ended artifact.

The reliable fix is detection at the point the record is finalized, treating a stub as a quality failure on the same footing as a hallucination. The gate matches the stub class on raw output, rejects any record containing one, tallies it under a new template_leak reason, and lets overgeneration backfill a clean record at no change to the delivered count. Rejecting rather than repairing is deliberate — a record containing such a stub is not a good document with one bad token; it is a template skeleton whose surrounding sentence is itself a stub. The gate is also deliberately narrow, leaving legitimate placeholders untouched: enumerated entities that disambiguate instances (DATE_1, CPT_CODE_2), domain-realistic identifiers, and descriptive field placeholders a document genuinely calls for. Adding a counted rejection reason changed the run manifest's shape, so its schema advanced from 1.1 to 1.2 — a documented event a buyer can see in a diff, rather than a silently added field.

04 — Operational Hardening

Isolating a hardware fault from the software on top of it

A pipeline is only as trustworthy as the substrate it runs on. The local substrate began aborting intermittently with a C-level memory fault — free(): invalid pointer, then Aborted (core dumped) — and the fault was non-deterministic. The same run, same inputs, would complete on one attempt and abort on another. Across one working session the log recorded seventy-one run starts against twenty-six clean completions, and the crash surfaced most often at the largest fresh allocation in the pipeline, which after the learned-NER tier was added became the GLiNER2 model load.

The temptation was to blame the most recently added software — the model load at whose moment the crash appeared. Resisting that was the first step of the diagnosis. A software defect in the model-load path would fail deterministically; this did not. Tested across process boundaries, resume and fresh runs, and batch sizes, the crashes and successes mixed with no software discriminator — the signature of marginal hardware surfacing under heavy allocation, not a code defect. The discipline was to make the fault measurable before attempting any fix: a controlled baseline established the failure rate at roughly thirty percent, so any candidate fix could be judged against it rather than declared successful after one lucky run.

Two software changes were made in this period — neither a fix, both correct hardening. The de-identification evaluation was moved into a subprocess, so a memory fault in the model load could no longer take down the parent process or a completed run; this rested on a durability property the pipeline already had, where each batch is written and fsync'd before the next begins, so --resume rebuilds the consolidated output crash-safe and duplicate-free. That contained the blast radius. It did not lower the crash rate, because the root cause was physical.

The host is an Intel Core i9-14900KF, within the population affected by the documented Intel 13th- and 14th-generation desktop instability — a Vmin Shift in a clock-tree circuit that ages under elevated voltage and temperature, whose symptom class is exactly this: intermittent, allocation-triggered faults that worsen with age. The resolution was the vendor remediation — a motherboard firmware update to MSI BIOS A.90 carrying Intel microcode revision 0x12B, applied with Intel's recommended default power settings. The fix is verified: nine consecutive full thousand-record runs across both substrates completed with zero faults, including the exact GLiNER2 model loads that had been the most reliable trigger. Against a measured thirty-percent pre-fix failure rate, nine clean completions is not a hopeful streak; it is a resolved fault.

05 — Economics

The fault was also a cost

The fault's most easily overlooked consequence was economic. On the cloud substrate, every mid-run abort forced a resume, and each resume paid another A10G cold-start and model load before generation could continue — inflating a run's realized cost above its true compute cost by the price of an extra cold start. The firmware fix, by letting runs complete in a single pass, removed that resume tax. Meanwhile a separate change — consolidating three repositories into one application around a shared core — raised a fair question of whether the abstraction cost anything per record. It did not: the substrate seam is a single backend protocol, not a translation layer in the inference path. The shared core supplies gating, provenance, evaluation, and delivery; each substrate generates directly.

Per-run cost was measured directly from the Modal credit balance, before and after each run. The honest representative figure is the most recent thousand-record run, which completed in a single pass with no resume:

That $0.47 lands squarely inside the original paper's measured band, which is the point: the earlier runs in the same series that cost more — roughly $0.55 to $0.84 — were not a different compute cost but the stability fault expressed in dollars. Reporting an average across them would overstate the truth. The no-resume run is the steady-state cost the system incurs now that runs complete reliably, and consolidation into one application preserved it exactly. The system is both one application and as cheap to run as it was as three.

06 — Delivery & Provenance

The machinery that makes the claims checkable

For a product whose differentiator is transparency, the packaging is not incidental — it is the form in which every claim above becomes verifiable. Each run writes one authoritative run_manifest.json, validated against a published JSON Schema by a conformance test so the documented shape and the emitted shape cannot drift. The manifest is self-describing: run identity and mode, the product and labeling axes, the full resolved configuration inline, a provenance object (git commit, branch, working-tree state, and the torch / transformers / vllm / modal / cuda versions), and a reconciliation object the pipeline verifies on load, so the manifest, the evaluation report, and the output file cannot disagree.

The delivery packager turns a completed run into a versioned SKU: a deterministic 70/20/10 train/validation/test split stratified by domain — reproducible from the run and seed rather than merely trusted — a free sample, product-aware data and summary cards rendered from the manifest, the evaluation report, a changelog, and a SHA-256 checksum manifest over every delivered file. Two properties carry the audit story: a dirty git working tree blocks a release unless explicitly overridden, so a deliverable cannot be cut from an unversioned code state by accident; and the checksum manifest lets a buyer verify the files received are bit-for-bit the files the manifest describes. The packaging is not separate from the product — it is how the product's central claim, that its labels are correct by construction and its quality is measured rather than asserted, is delivered to someone who must verify it before use.

07 — Limitations

Stated as plainly as the results

This work inherits every limitation the original paper states and adds a few specific to it. The premium tier's precision cost is real and concentrated: the 141 false positives on the two gap types are a genuine cost, not a free improvement, and a consumer needing maximal name precision should weight the base tier accordingly. The gap set itself is empirical, not derived — found by measurement on this product's domains — so a new domain with a different free-text entity type could shift which types the learned model strictly improves, and the right gap set there would have to be re-established rather than assumed. The template-leak gate is calibrated to stub forms seen to date, a reliable catch for a known artifact class but not a proof that no novel shape can pass. And the hardware resolution is verified for this specific host against a measured baseline; it is not a general claim about the processor family. The lesson there is methodological — how a non-deterministic fault was isolated from software and root-caused — rather than a universal hardware verdict.

That is the shape of the addendum. Construction-true labeling gives the de-identification product a ground truth no detector's recall ceiling bounds; the learned-NER tier measures the closure of that gap and earns its scope by failing twice first; the template-leak gate removes an emergent defect where prevention is actually possible; and the hardening effort restored both reliability and the measured cost structure beneath it. The thread connecting them is the one the first paper began with: a claim is worth no more than the evidence and the bounds stated alongside it.
